## IV. The Sorting Hat — What This Reveals

The algorithm has Loc's number. **45% of taps go to X threads** —
and the X threads it serves are *tightly* themed: Manchester United transfers,
AI announcements, programming hot-takes, the occasional Vietnam-watching tech
thread. The feed is not exposing him to a wider world; it is *concentrating*
his existing world.

After X, the next layer is **29% tech press** — Verge, TechCrunch,
Ars, 9to5, Tom's Guide. This is craftsman keeping pace, same as Chrome.
Then **5% Vietnamese news** (mostly e.vnexpress, the English-language
VnExpress) and **11% world press** (NYT, Atlantic, Economist,
Guardian — the broadsheets) — these are the ones that *do* reach beyond the
trade.

The honest read: Discover is *not* the secret door to wider reading. It's a
slightly different mirror of the same person. Football fan, AI watcher,
Vietnam citizen, occasional broadsheet reader. The breadth is real but small.

## V. What This Cannot Show

- **Discover history is short.** My Activity returned **400 days**;
  taps before 30 March 2025 aren't here. Google retains Discover
  taps for ~12–14 months by default.
- **"N cards in your feed" without a tap is invisible to taste.** The
  algorithm shows hundreds of cards every day; we only see the 352 taps,
  not the thousands of scrolls past.
- **Some entries say "Content From Discover" with no title** — Google
  redacts those for content/copyright reasons. Counted in the totals but
  excluded from the article list.
- **Read depth is unknown.** Tapping an X tweet is not the same as reading
  a 30-minute Atlantic essay; both count as one row.
- **iOS Google app only.** This is the Discover feed inside the iOS Google
  app, which is separate from Chrome history (already analyzed in The Owlery).
  Articles read via Safari, social-app webviews, or on desktop don't appear.
