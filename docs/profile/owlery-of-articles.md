## V. The Sorting Hat — What This Reading Reveals

This is a year of **deep, narrow reading dressed in occasional curiosity**.
The largest subject — **🪄 Software Craft** at 22% — is the
engineer's daily craft: documentation, framework changelogs, edge-case
answers on Stack Overflow. The next layer is the **AI frontier** —
every Gemini announcement, every Claude SDK doc, every leaderboard
check on lmarena. The watcher is keeping pace with a fast-moving
field, not surveying it from outside.

Beneath the dev/AI core, the reading thins into known loyalties:
**Vietnamese news** (16% — VnExpress, Tuoi Tre,
Thanh Nien), **fish-keeping forums and shops**
(3%), and the always-on **Wikipedia rabbit hole**
(7%) where the wider world actually peeks through.

**The Wikipedia tabs**, in particular, are the truest "from everywhere":
in the same year the watcher visited articles for "John Nolan (British actor)" · "77th Primetime Emmy Awards" · "Economy of Vietnam" · "The Odyssey (2026 film)" · "Hell Let Loose: Vietnam", plus the
Vietnamese-language entries on "29 tháng 4" · "Con ma nhà họ Hứa" · "Danh sách Thủ tướng Chính phủ Việt Nam" · "Harry Potter và Hoàng tử lai" · "Harry Potter". Cinema and football
and norse-mythology and Vietnamese history-and-folklore — all from one
midnight tab-spree, none of it for work.

The honest truth in the data: **"I read articles from everywhere"**
isn't quite right *by volume*. The bulk is craft. But the breadth IS
real — **448 distinct rare hosts** in the long tail,
each visited once or twice, scattered across the genuine width of the
web. The breadth lives in the corners, not the volume.

If the Sorting Hat had to say: **Ravenclaw with a Hufflepuff hobby
shelf**. A craftsman first — frameworks, AI papers, Stack Overflow at
3 PM — but a wanderer at midnight, opening Wikipedia tabs about extinct
film studios and Norse gods and 10th-century Vietnamese warlords until
the laptop closes itself.

## VI. What This Cannot Show

- **Time horizon.** Takeout's Chrome export is sync-only and capped
  at roughly 364 days for this account. Anything before 4 May 2025 is invisible.
- **Reading depth.** A title in this list could mean a 3-second tab
  close or a 30-minute deep read. Chrome history doesn't record dwell.
- **Other devices.** Articles read on phone or tablet (without sync)
  don't appear here.
- **Incognito.** Anything opened in private mode is invisible.
- **Work URLs leaked through.** The filter dropped 79,872
  of 83,850 entries as work/utility/auth/short-title — but a few
  Papaya/Cassava/internal pages may still be in the candidate set if
  they had article-style titles.
- **Title bias.** Classification reads page titles, not article body.
  An article about Korean demographics on businessinsider.com might
  cluster under "Tech Discussion" simply because of the host.
